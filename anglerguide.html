Reading the Water

A Bass Angler's Guide to Lure Selection

Every angler has stood at the tackle box, stared at rows of lures, and wondered which one to tie on. It feels like guesswork. And for beginners, it often is. But experienced bass anglers know that lure selection follows a logic rooted in how bass sense their environment. Three variables drive almost every good decision: water clarity, lure type, and retrieve techniques. Get those three right, and you give yourself a real chance.

How Bass See Your Lure

Bass are primarily sight predators. In water with eight or more feet of visibility, bass hunt and feed almost entirely by sight (Slamming Bass). That changes everything about how you choose a lure color.

The most fundamental rule is to fish brightly colored baits in dingy or muddy water and use light, subtle colors in clear water (Bourne, Bass Pro Shops 1Source). The reasoning is straightforward. In clear water, bass have time to inspect your lure closely, and anything unnatural will get refused. In murky water, they can barely see it, so contrast matters more than realism. Dark and solid colors like black, blue, and dark purple create a strong silhouette that bass can detect more easily in low-visibility conditions (Omnia Fishing).

Water clarity is not the only factor. Depth affects color the same way clarity does: the deeper a lure runs, the less light reaches it, and colors fade. Reds and oranges disappear first; blues and greens hold their color longest at depth (Kraken Bass). This is why a chartreuse crankbait that looks gaudy in your hand can be nearly invisible fifteen feet down on a sunny afternoon.

Both water clarity and light penetration matter, but water clarity is the dominating factor (Jones and Roland, BassResource). A cloudy day with clear water still calls for natural colors. Bright sun over muddy water still calls for high-contrast lures. The water tells you more than the sky does.

Color is not decoration. It is communication.

Forage matching adds another layer on top of clarity. If the main forage in a lake is crawfish, green pumpkin, brown, and orange are the colors bass expect to see. If bass are chasing shad, white, silver, and chrome mimic the flash of a baitfish (Kraken Bass).

Choosing the Right Lure Type

Once you have a color direction, you need to decide what kind of lure to throw. There are dozens of lure categories, but seven cover the vast majority of bass fishing situations: crankbaits, jigs, soft plastics, spinnerbaits, topwaters, jerkbaits, and swimbaits.

Crankbaits are the workhorse search tool. Shallow squarebills cover the banks in two to eight feet of water. Deep-divers reach suspended fish at fifteen to twenty feet. In stained water, choose a crankbait with a rattle. In cold water, slow down and use a tighter wobble (FishingBooker). Crankbaits reward anglers who cover water efficiently.

View crankbait diagram

Jigs are arguably the most versatile lure ever made. They mimic crawfish, one of the largemouth bass's preferred food sources (FishingBooker). They can be flipped into heavy cover, dragged across the bottom, or swum through the water column. Jigs work all year, but summer and spring are when they tend to shine most (Norrik). They require more patience and feel than other lures, but the trade-off is access to places where bass hide that no crankbait can reach.

View jig diagram

Soft plastics are the great equalizer. They work well in a variety of water conditions: clear or muddy, with cover or without (Take Me Fishing). The Texas-rigged worm remains one of the most effective bass setups ever developed, particularly in warm water when bass are relating to bottom structure. Green pumpkin is the consensus starting color because it works almost everywhere.

View soft plastic diagram

Spinnerbaits are reaction lures built for flash and vibration. They work great in areas with lily pads, stumps, logs, and other hard structure because they are designed to deflect off cover (Take Me Fishing). The spinning blade creates both visual attraction and a thumping pressure wave that bass can detect through their lateral line, which matters in low-visibility water where sight alone is not enough.

View spinnerbait diagram

Topwater lures are fished right on the surface and produce some of the most exciting strikes in bass fishing. Poppers use a cupped face to throw water and create a popping sound. Walking baits glide side to side in a zigzag pattern called "walking the dog." Topwaters are most effective in low-light conditions like early morning and late evening, or when bass are actively feeding near the surface (FishingBooker).

View topwater diagram

Jerkbaits are slim, minnow-shaped hard baits with a small lip that gives them a subtle diving action. What makes them special is their ability to suspend at a set depth when you stop reeling. The technique is a rhythmic twitch-pause-twitch with the rod tip, and the pause is where strikes happen. Jerkbaits shine in cooler water when bass are less willing to chase fast-moving lures (BassForecast).

View jerkbait diagram

Swimbaits are designed to look and move like real baitfish. Soft-bodied swimbaits have a paddletail that kicks side to side on a steady retrieve, creating a natural swimming action. They come in a wide range of sizes, from small three-inch models for finesse fishing to large eight-inch versions for targeting trophy bass. Swimbaits work best in clear to slightly stained water where bass are feeding on shad or other baitfish (Wired2Fish).

Retrieve Techniques

You can have the right color and the right lure and still catch nothing because of how you work it. Retrieve is the most overlooked variable for beginners, and often the most important one once the fundamentals are in place.

A straight retrieve is the most basic approach: cast, reel, and let the lure's built-in action do the work (Bass Pro Shops 1Source). Crankbaits and spinnerbaits are designed for this. The key adjustment is speed. Fast retrieves trigger reaction strikes from aggressive fish. Slow retrieves give lethargic or cold-water bass time to commit.

The stop-and-go retrieve adds a pause to the straight retrieve. Suspending crankbaits and jerkbaits are ideal for this approach because they hold their depth when you stop reeling (BassForecast). The pause imitates a wounded baitfish that loses momentum, which is often when bass strike. If fish are following but not biting, adding a pause usually changes that.

For bottom-contact lures like jigs and soft plastics, the lift-and-drop is the core technique. You raise the rod slowly, lifting the lure off the bottom and pulling it forward, then lower the rod back and reel up the slack (Wired2Fish). Most strikes happen on the fall, so slack line management is essential. A twitch in the line often means a fish has taken the lure before it hits bottom.

The most successful anglers do not get stuck on one retrieve. They adapt throughout the day, responding to how fish are behaving and where they are holding (Sport Fishing Magazine). If the fish are telling you something is not working, change one variable at a time. Slow down first. Then adjust the pause. Then change the lure. That systematic approach teaches you the water faster than randomly switching lures ever will.

Putting It Together

Lure selection starts to feel intuitive once you understand what each decision is actually doing. Color talks to visibility. Lure type talks to depth, cover, and forage. Retrieve talks to fish mood and activity level. None of these decisions exist in isolation.

The water tells you more than the sky does.

The interactive guide paired with this essay lets you work through these variables yourself. Set the water clarity, pick a scenario, and see which combinations make sense and why. The goal is not to give you a magic answer for every situation. It is to build the pattern recognition that experienced anglers develop over years on the water. The more you understand the reasoning, the faster that intuition develops.
